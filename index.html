Nuclear Energy: A Non-Renewable Source

How it Works

Nuclear energy is generated by splitting uranium atoms through a process called fission. When a neutron collides with a uranium-235 atom, both split, releasing a large amount of energy as heat and radiation. This heat warms a cooling agent (typically water) to produce steam, which then spins large turbines connected to an electric generator, producing electricity.

Why it's Non-Renewable

Nuclear energy is considered non-renewable because its primary fuel, Uranium-235, is a finite resource. It cannot be replenished naturally within a timeframe relevant to human use. Uranium is found in rocks globally, but its supply is limited.

Impacts of Nuclear Energy

Societal Impact

  • Provides well-paying, long-lasting employment opportunities.
  • Offers a stable energy supply that won't deplete quickly.
  • Challenge: Rare accidents can release dangerous radiation, leading to radiation poisoning or cancer.
  • Challenge: In severe accidents, communities may be forced to evacuate their homes due to safety concerns.
  • Effects of radiation exposure can last for years if not properly treated.

Environmental Impact

  • Benefit: Does not release greenhouse gases (like carbon dioxide) into the atmosphere, contributing to less air pollution and climate change compared to fossil fuels.
  • Challenge: Produces highly dangerous radioactive waste that remains hazardous for thousands of years, requiring secure underground storage to prevent contamination of water and soil.
  • Building nuclear plants requires significant materials and energy.
  • Challenge: Major accidents (e.g., Chernobyl) can severely harm local ecosystems, affecting water, plants, and animals for extended periods.

Economic Impact

  • Average cost of a 2,200 MW nuclear reactor ranges from \$7,675 to \$12,500 per kilowatt.
  • Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) varies from \$129 to \$198/MWh.
  • Fuel costs are significantly lower than fossil fuels:
    • 1/3 to 1/2 of coal-fired plants.
    • 1/4 to 1/5 of gas combined-cycle plants.
  • For comparison:
    • Coal LCOE: \$60 to \$143/MWh.
    • Gas Combined Cycle LCOE: \$65 to \$159/MWh.

Hydroelectric Energy: A Renewable Source

How it Works

Hydroelectric power harnesses the energy of flowing water, typically through a dam, to generate electricity. Water is stored in artificial lakes or reservoirs, increasing its potential energy. When released through tunnels (penstocks), the water strikes the blades of a turbine, causing it to spin. The spinning turbine drives a generator, producing Alternating Current (AC) electricity. Transformers then convert this electricity to a higher voltage for transmission to consumers. Some systems also use a "run-of-river" approach, harnessing natural river flow without a large dam.

Why it's Renewable

Hydroelectric energy is renewable because it relies on the continuous water cycle, which is powered by solar energy. The processes of evaporation, condensation, and precipitation ensure a constant supply of water for hydropower. The water used in hydroelectric plants is returned to the water cycle, making the process sustainable.

Impacts of Hydroelectric Energy

Societal Impact

  • Challenge: Can increase neurotoxin methylmercury concentrations in food webs, particularly impacting nearby Indigenous communities.
  • Flooding of land for hydroelectric projects can convert microbes in soils to mercury, which then enters the food chain.
  • High risk of exposure for those consuming locally caught fish, birds, and marine animals, especially pregnant women and children.
  • Methylmercury exposure can increase the risk of cardiovascular disease and neurodevelopmental delays in children.
  • Impacts employment opportunities as health issues can lead to less education and lower employment rates.

Environmental Impact

  • Canada uses 9% of the global water supply for hydropower, producing 60% of its electricity.
  • Challenge: Dams can alter water temperature and oxygen levels, negatively impacting fish habitats.
  • Challenge: Can lead to the destruction of nearby plant and animal life due to flooding and habitat alteration.
  • Many older hydropower plants did not adequately consider the local land and environment during their construction.
  • Building hydroelectric dams is costly and time-consuming (e.g., Centrale Robert-Bourassa cost \$3.8 billion and took 7 years).

Economic Impact

  • Cost of producing electricity is low in Canada once the power plant and dam are constructed.
  • Hydroelectricity costs around \$30 to \$60/MWh.
  • Highly competitive compared to other sources:
    • Beats coal (\$60-\$100/MWh).
    • Beats wind (\$40-\$75/MWh).
    • Beats solar (\$50-\$100/MWh).
  • Provinces like Quebec and Manitoba have lower electricity costs due to their reliance on hydroelectricity.
  • Despite high initial construction costs, the long-term low operating costs make it economically worthwhile.

Canada's Energy Future: A Balanced Approach

Both nuclear and hydroelectric energy sources play crucial roles in Canada's current and future energy landscape. A balanced approach, leveraging the strengths of each, is essential for meeting energy demands and achieving sustainability goals.

Current Roles

  • Nuclear Energy: Provides essential baseload power, ensuring a steady supply of electricity to the grid. This stability is vital for integrating intermittent renewable sources like solar and wind. Canada's CANDU reactors have reliably supplied clean energy for decades.
  • Hydroelectric Energy: Forms the backbone of Canada's electricity grid. With over 500 facilities, it provides more than 63% of the country's power from renewable sources. It has been a reliable energy source for nearly 150 years, known for its low GHG emissions, making it a clean, reliable, and renewable option.

Future Roles

  • Nuclear Energy: Expected to be crucial in achieving Canada's net-zero goals by 2050, offering a reliable and carbon-free energy source. Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) are being explored for their potential to power electrical grids, replace fossil fuels in industrial processes, and provide energy in off-grid settings.
  • Hydroelectric Energy: Hydro projects are anticipated to continue supporting the growth of industry, commerce, infrastructure, and communities. Significant investments (over \$125 billion) and job creation (a million jobs) are projected over the next 20 years, further solidifying its role.

Given their respective strengths and contributions, both nuclear and hydroelectric methods should continue to be utilized in Canada's energy portfolio. While hydroelectricity remains a dominant and clean baseload, nuclear energy, especially with advancements like SMRs, offers a crucial carbon-free complement for stability and meeting future demands.
